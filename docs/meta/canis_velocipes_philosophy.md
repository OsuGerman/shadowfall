# Mechanische Loyalität
## A Visual Philosophy

### The Movement

**Mechanische Loyalität** — *Mechanical Loyalty* — is a design movement that treats the most affectionate of subjects with the rigour of an engineering plate. It refuses sentiment and finds tenderness instead in the certainty of a perfect circle, the honesty of an unmodulated colour field, the inevitability of a triangle resting on its base. It is the Bauhaus's vocabulary of pure form turned toward a quietly absurd, deeply human subject — and it carries the gravity of an archive catalogue compiled over decades by a single, patient hand.

### Form & Geometry

Every element resolves to a primary geometric primitive: the circle, the triangle, the line, the arc. Nothing is rendered — everything is **constructed**. Curves are drawn with the discipline of a compass; verticals plumb true; intersections meet at calculated angles. The composition reads as if it were typeset on a draftsman's board after a hundred preparatory sketches, each one a refinement of the last. There is no gesture without measurement. There is no shape without intention. The result must feel inevitable — the only possible arrangement of these elements, decided by a master who knew the rule by which all others were excluded.

### Colour & Material

The palette is severely limited and historically grounded: warm ivory paper as the ground; deep ink-black for structure; a single saturated vermillion that carries the chromatic weight of the entire composition; an ochre or mustard accent used sparingly as counterpoint; a single cool blue reserved for the rarest emphasis. Colour is never decorative — it is **information**. Each field is flat, opaque, edge-perfect, with the subtle grain of letterpress impression rather than the slickness of digital print. The paper itself must read as material: aged but cared-for, the colour of an institutional almanac that has rested on a shelf for a century.

### Composition & Rhythm

A strong central axis governs the page. The principal motif sits on this axis with the formal stillness of an icon. Around it: vast, intentional silence — negative space treated as the most expensive material in the composition. Thin rules — hairlines of one or two pixels — divide the canvas into measured zones, each zone earning its content. Small typographic markers in the margins (a plate number, a tick, a cardinal letter) suggest a larger catalogue system of which this page is one of many — the work of countless hours by a cataloguer at the absolute top of their field.

### Typography as Specimen Label

Text appears only where the eye demands an anchor, and never as explanation. Serifs reserved for the dignified principal label — a single Latinate inscription, set small, leaded generously, all caps with archival restraint. A monospaced or geometric sans handles the marginalia: serial numbers, plate numbers, a date in Roman numerals. Typography is never decoration — it is the **caption a museum curator would write**, weighed against silence. Nothing shouts. Nothing falls off the page. Every glyph is placed with painstaking attention.

### Spirit

The final work must look as though it were extracted from a folio of forgotten technical drawings — an artefact from an imaginary institute that catalogued, with monastic patience, the formal grammar of devotion. It must feel meticulously crafted, the product of deep expertise, master-level execution. The viewer should sense the labour: the unseen hundreds of hours behind every certain line. Those who recognise the formal echoes — Rodchenko, Lissitzky, Bayer, Bill, Müller-Brockmann, Duchamp's spinning wheel — will feel the lineage. Those who don't will simply experience an object of austere, undeniable beauty.
